Tall narrow container. Garlic, egg whites, lime juice, salt. Blitz until the garlic goes from sharp-smelling to mellow—that's when you know it's actually ready. Then comes the oil, dripped in slow while the blender never stops, and suddenly you've got garlic mayonnaise that feels homemade because it is. Twenty minutes total. Most of that is just standing there watching it thicken.

## Why You'll Love This Garlic Mayo Sauce

Makes enough to last a week. Tastes nothing like jarred stuff—actual garlic, actual tang from the lime.

Grilling season, especially. Brush it on fish before it hits the grill. Dip vegetables into it cold. Works on sandwiches, in eggs, under roasted chicken skin.

One bowl, one blender. No whisking by hand until your arm dies.

Thickens more as it sits. Refrigerated, it gets almost spreadable. Some people like it thin the first day. I don't. Give it overnight.

## Garlic Mayonnaise Recipe Ingredients

Four garlic cloves, quartered. Not minced—quartered. Bigger pieces blend smoother somehow.

Two large egg whites. Room temperature works better, but cold works fine too. The yolk stays out—this is why it emulsifies so clean.

Fresh lime juice. Twenty-five milliliters. Lemon works. White vinegar works. But lime stays bright without being sharp.

Sea salt. Three milliliters. Coarse kind. Dissolves into the sauce instead of sitting on your tongue.

Sunflower oil. Three hundred fifty milliliters. Olive oil's too aggressive—burns the flavor. Avocado oil works. Vegetable oil works. Just not olive.

Water. Forty-five milliliters. Thins it out mid-emulsion so you don't end up with something that won't blend anymore. Sounds weird. Matters.

Smoked paprika. Optional. Two milliliters. Gives it a twist if you want one. Grilling sauce, really.

## How to Make Garlic Mayonnaise Sauce

Get a tall, narrow container. Wide containers let air in—breaks the emulsion. Doesn't have to be fancy. A pint glass works.

Throw the garlic in, then egg whites, then lime juice and salt. If you're using the paprika, add it now.

Stick the hand blender all the way down—fully submerged. Blitz hard. The garlic dissolves into actual pulp, stops being chunks. Takes maybe two minutes. Smell it. Sharp garlic smell goes soft and almost sweet. That's your signal.

Keep the blender going. Medium speed. Start drizzling the oil. Not pouring. Drizzling. Like you're being careful with it, which you are. A thin steady stream—the sauce thickens almost instantly, starts looking like actual mayo right in front of you. Feels like magic every time.

## How to Get Garlic Mayonnaise Dip Thick and Creamy

Once you've got half the oil in and it's holding together, stop being so stingy. Alternate now—splash of water, then a bit more oil. Water. Oil. Water. Oil. Keeps the blender from working too hard, keeps the emulsion stable.

Watch the color shift. Gets glossy, then thick, then it starts clinging to the spoon with a gentle wobble. That's when you're done adding stuff.

Taste it. This is important. Add more salt if it's flat. Add paprika if you want smoke. Blend five more seconds—don't blend for longer than you need to or it can break on you.

Too thin? One more spoonful of oil, blended slow. Don't rush it.

## Garlic Mayo Dip Tips and Common Mistakes

Breaks happen. Curdled, separated, looks like it lost its mind. Don't throw it away. Crack a fresh egg white into a new container, start blending that egg white alone for maybe thirty seconds, then slowly—and I mean slowly—whisk the broken sauce in. Works almost every time. Not sure why the second egg white fixes it. Just does.

Grilled fish needs it cold. Vegetables need it cold. Sandwiches work room temp or cold—doesn't matter. Stays good refrigerated for about a week. Thickens more as it sits. Some people think that means it's gone bad. It hasn't.

Don't use hot ingredients. Cold or room temp, everything. Hot breaks emulsions.

Blender speed matters more than people think. Too high, it breaks. Medium. Steady. Not a race.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Garlic Mayonnaise Sauce

**Can I use whole garlic cloves instead of quartered?**
Blends slower. Takes longer. Ends up with tiny pieces sometimes. Quartered gives you a smoother result because there's more surface area hitting the blades at once.

**What if my garlic mayo sauce breaks while I'm making it?**
Start over with a fresh egg white in a new container, blend that egg white alone for thirty seconds, then slowly whisk in the broken batch. Fixes it almost every time.

**Does homemade garlic mayo taste different than store-bought?**
Completely different. The garlic stays bright and fresh. Jarred stuff has gone through stuff to preserve it. This one tastes like what it actually is.

**How long does homemade garlic aioli keep?**
One week, refrigerated in an airtight container. Thickens more as it chills—that's fine, normal, not spoiling.

**Can I use egg yolks instead of egg whites for garlic mayo dip?**
Yolks make it richer, heavier. Whites keep it lighter, brighter. Try it either way. Yolks emulsify faster though.

**Should I use cold or room temperature ingredients?**
Room temp works best, but cold works fine. Hot breaks it. Don't use hot anything.

**What oil works best for garlic mayonnaise recipe?**
Sunflower, avocado, vegetable. Not olive—too much flavor. Not anything toasted or dark. Neutral oil lets the garlic do the talking.

**Can I make this without a hand blender?**
Not really. A regular blender works but the container's too wide. A food processor works if you keep it moving. Whisking by hand? Takes forever, doesn't emulsify the same way.
