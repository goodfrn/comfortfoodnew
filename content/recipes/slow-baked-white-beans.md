## Why You'll Love This Slow-Baked White Beans Dish

Takes forever but you're not doing anything. Dutch oven goes in the oven and stays there.
Comfort food that doesn't feel heavy. Beans get creamy without any cream.
One bowl, one egg, bread if you want it. Nothing else required.
Works great the next day cold or reheated—tastes even better, kind of like it sat and figured itself out.
Vegetarian but doesn't taste like you're missing meat. The egg fixes that.

## What You Need for Slow-Baked White Beans with Eggs

Dried lima beans or cannellini—428 grams, whatever you've got. Rinsed. Don't skip this or it's gritty.

Onion. One medium one, chopped fine. Not thin slices. Fine. The heat breaks it down completely.

Extra virgin olive oil. 50 ml to start, then more at the end. The good kind. Cheap oil tastes thin here.

Parmesan rind or pecorino. The hard piece nobody eats. This is what it's for. Melts into nothing, leaves flavor behind.

Fresh thyme. Three sprigs. Dried doesn't work the same. Not close.

Broth. Chicken or vegetable. 1.5 liters. Has to be real broth—not bouillon, not the thin stuff. It becomes the sauce.

Garlic. Two cloves. Whole. Unpeeled. They go soft and mild this way, nothing sharp.

Nutmeg. Quarter teaspoon, freshly grated. Ground tastes like dust. Whole nutmeg takes thirty seconds.

Chili flakes. Quarter teaspoon. Optional if you hate heat, but it doesn't make it spicy—just alive.

Lemon zest. From one lemon. Brightens everything at the end.

Eggs. Four of them. Large. Soft-boiled exactly six minutes.

Salt and pepper. Fleur de sel or coarse sea salt. The kind you can see and taste.

## How to Make Slow-Baked White Beans

Start the night before. Dump beans in a bowl, cover with cold water. Twelve hours minimum. They float at first, then sink. Just making sure they're hydrated all the way through.

Heat your oven to 160°C. Mid-level rack. Matters more than you'd think.

Get a Dutch oven or heavy pot with a lid—something that goes stovetop to oven without complaining. Warm the olive oil over medium heat. Not hot. Medium.

Chop the onion. Add it to the warm oil. Don't stir constantly. Let it sit for 10 minutes, maybe 11. You're waiting for golden edges to show up. That's when the sweetness happens. Patient onions taste different than rushed ones.

Add the Parmesan rind, thyme, your drained beans, and the broth. Stir it together. Put the heat up just enough to get it boiling—real boiling, not a simmer. Watch for the foam that rises to the top. Skim it off with a slotted spoon. Twice usually does it.

Drop in the garlic cloves—whole, unpeeled, don't mess with them. Sprinkle nutmeg and chili flakes over the surface. Pinch of salt now, but restrain yourself. You'll taste it after.

## How to Get Slow-Baked White Beans Creamy and Tender

Cover the pot. Transfer it directly to the oven. Don't fuss. The oven does the work now.

One hour. Check them. Push a bean between your thumb and forefinger. Still whole? Good. Still hard? Weird. Let it go another ten minutes max. They should start yielding without falling apart entirely.

The broth thickens as it cooks. That's not flour—that's starch from the beans. The broth becomes silky. Rich. That's the goal.

Pull it out. Keep the lid on. Let it sit for 15 minutes. Covered. The residual heat finishes the beans without killing them.

Remove the Parmesan rind—it's flavorless now, just peel it out. Remove the thyme sprigs. The garlic cloves are soft enough that you can squeeze them out of their skins back into the pot using a small spoon or just your fingers. It works.

Taste it. Now you season. Salt, pepper, taste again. The lemon zest went in early, but if it needs more brightness, squeeze fresh lemon juice in now. Not the zest—the juice. Different thing.

## Soft-Boiled Eggs and White Beans Serving Tips

While the beans rest, fill a small pot with water. Bring it to a gentle simmer. Not a boil. Simmer means active steam, no rolling bubbles throwing eggs around.

Lower the eggs in gently using a spoon. This matters. Cold eggs in hot water crack sometimes. Use the spoon. Six minutes exactly. Not five, not seven. Six. That's soft whites, runny yolks. That's the point.

When six minutes hits, lift them out with a slotted spoon. Ice-cold water immediately. Two minutes in there. Stops the cooking. Peels easier this way.

Peel under running cold water. Cold makes the membrane let go. Warm eggs stick to their shells like they're mad at you.

Pat them dry. Gently. They're fragile.

Beans go in shallow bowls. One egg on top of each bowl. Drizzle olive oil over everything. The good oil. This is where it shows up. Fleur de sel, cracked black pepper. That's the whole thing.

Optional: blanched greens on the side. Spinach. Chard. Something you can push the egg yolk into. Crusty bread rubbed with a raw garlic clove for soaking up broth. Parmesan shavings if you want extra umami. Nobody will stop you.

## Frequently Asked Questions About White Beans with Eggs

**Can you skip the overnight soaking?**
Technically no. Soaked beans cook evenly. Unsoaked beans have hard centers even after hours in the oven. Not worth it.

**What if you don't have a Dutch oven?**
Any heavy pot with a lid that tolerates 160°C works. Cast iron. Enameled. Doesn't matter. It just needs to go from stovetop to oven without cracking or melting.

**Can you use canned beans instead?**
Don't. Canned beans turn to mush at these temperatures. They're already soft. This recipe needs something to work with. Start from dried.

**What's the Parmesan rind actually doing?**
Dissolving into the broth. Umami. The rind is mostly gelatin and salt and flavor compounds. Adds depth. You can skip it if you're out, but the dish tastes thinner without it.

**Why exactly six minutes for the eggs?**
Six minutes gets you runny yolks and set whites that still give slightly when you poke them. Five minutes and the white is still soft in spots. Seven and the yolk starts turning pale. Six is the sweet spot. Set a timer.

**Can you make this in a slow cooker instead of the oven?**
Yeah, actually. Low for five to six hours. Same result. Maybe slightly less control over the beans not falling apart, but close enough. Same taste. Slower cooker, slower result. Makes sense.

**Does the lemon zest have to go in during cooking?**
Mostly yes. Fresh zest loses brightness as it cooks. Early zest gets mellow and integrated. Late juice tastes sharp and separate. Both have their place, but the zest belongs in the pot. Add juice at the end if it needs it.
