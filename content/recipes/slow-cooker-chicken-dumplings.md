ingredients:
- "3 tablespoons unsalted butter"
- "2 cans condensed cream of mushroom soup"
- "3 cups chicken broth"
- "1 can water"
- "1 medium yellow onion chopped fine"
- "3 boneless skinless chicken breasts"
- "Half cup all purpose flour"
- "5 tablespoons ice cold water"
- "Salt and pepper to taste"
instructions:
- "Put the butter, mushroom soup, chicken broth, water, and chopped onion in the slow cooker. Lay the chicken breasts in and push them down until they're fully covered by liquid. If they're sticking out, pour in a bit more broth. Chicken that sits above the liquid dries out and goes tough."
- "Set it to low. Walk away for 7 hours. The broth thickens on its own as the soup and butter melt together. By hour five or six the whole kitchen smells like it. The chicken will be so tender it barely holds its shape."
- "About an hour before you want to eat, switch to high. Take a fork or a potato masher and break the chicken into chunky pieces right inside the pot. You want actual bites of chicken in there, not a shredded paste. If the meat doesn't break apart easy, give it more time on low. It's not ready."
- "While the chicken heats on high, make the dumplings. Half a cup of flour and five tablespoons of ice cold water in a bowl. Stir it until it comes together into a dough. It should feel firm enough to roll but still a little soft. If it sticks to your hands add a touch more flour. If it cracks and falls apart add a tiny bit more water."
- "Dust your counter with flour and roll the dough out thin. About an eighth of an inch. Thinner dumplings cook through properly and don't end up with that raw doughy center. Cut them into rough squares with a pizza cutter or a knife. They don't need to be even."
- "Drop the dumplings around the edges of the slow cooker. The sides run hotter than the middle. Give them one gentle stir so they get coated in the broth but don't push them all under. They cook best when they're partly in the liquid and partly sitting on top."
- "Put the lid back on. Leave it on high for 45 to 50 minutes. Don't open it during that time. The steam trapped inside is what cooks the dumplings through. When they're done they'll feel springy when you press one. Pull one out and cut it in half if you're not sure. No raw flour taste means they're good."
- "Taste the broth. Add salt and pepper. Let the whole thing sit covered for another 10 minutes before you serve it. The broth thickens a bit more as the dumplings soak it up."
introduction: "Made this for the first time during a snowstorm. Couldn't go out. Had chicken and mushroom soup lying around. Threw it in the slow cooker and hoped for the best. Came out better than anything I'd made on the stove. That was years ago and I haven't changed the recipe since. The mushroom soup and butter do something together in there. After a few hours it turns into this thick creamy broth without you adding any cream. Chicken sits in it all day and by the end it's falling apart. Then you make dumplings. Flour. Cold water. That's it. I was nervous the first time because I thought homemade dough was going to be a whole thing. It took two minutes. Roll thin. Cut squares. Drop them in. Done in under an hour. I used cream of chicken for a while. It was ok. Mushroom is better. More flavor, less boring. Stovetop feels like a lot of work now compared to this."
ingredientsNote: "Butter right at the start. Melts slow, works its way into everything. I use mushroom soup. Most people use cream of chicken. Mushroom has more going on. Either works though. The broth has to cover the chicken all the way or you end up with dry tough spots on top. Happened to me once. Annoying. Onion chopped small so it kind of dissolves into the sauce. For the dumplings it's flour and ice water. Cold is the key. Room temp water and the gluten tightens up. Dumplings come out chewy and heavy. Nobody wants that. The dough should hold together but not stick to everything. No baking powder. Sounds weird but the steam in the cooker handles it."
instructionsNote: "Everything in. Low. All day. Walk away and do something else. The butter and soup do their thing over time and the broth thickens up without you touching it. Don't break the chicken early. Tried it. Bad idea. Meat went stringy and weird. Proteins need the full cook time before you mess with them. Last hour, crank it to high, then break it up. Chunky. Not fine. Dumplings go near the sides. Middle of the slow cooker is the cool zone. Edges are where the heat builds up. That's where they cook right. Stir once so they're coated. Then lid on and don't touch it. Every time you open it you let the steam out and the dumplings need that steam. Canned biscuits work too if you cut them up. Different thing. More bready. But fine for a weeknight."
tips:
- "Full 7 hours on low before you do anything. Don't peek. You know it's getting there by the smell. Broth should look thicker and the edges of the chicken will be coming apart on their own. Then high heat and break the meat. Fork or masher. Big pieces."
- "Thin dumplings. That's the trick. Eighth of an inch. Roll them out and cut with a pizza cutter. Thick ones look cooked outside but they're gummy and raw in the middle."
- "Sides of the slow cooker. Not the center. That's where dumplings actually cook through. Half in the broth half out. Don't shove them under or they turn to mush."
- "Cold water for the dough. Has to be cold. Makes the dumplings soft. Warm water does the opposite. No baking powder in this. Steam under the lid puffs them up enough."
- "Fridge 3 days. Dumplings drink up all the liquid overnight so add broth when you reheat. Stove, low, careful stirring. Microwave turns them into rubber. Freezes ok for a couple months."
faq:
- "q: When to break up the chicken? a: Last hour after switching to high. Fork should slide through. If you're having to pull at it then it's not there yet. Doing it early wrecks the texture. Learned that one the hard way."
- "q: Canned biscuits instead of homemade? a: Sure. Quarter them. Drop around the edges same as regular dumplings. More bready, less tender. Some people like it better actually."
- "q: Mushroom soup or cream of chicken? a: I go mushroom every time. More depth. Cream of chicken is traditional though and it works. Broth comes out creamy either way."
- "q: Dumplings gummy inside? a: Too thick. Thinner next time. And at the edges not the middle. 50 minutes on high. Maybe 10 more if your cooker runs cool."
- "q: Leftovers? a: Cool it. Container. Fridge. Add broth reheating because the dumplings soak everything up. Stove is best. Gentle heat. Freezes a couple months."
- "q: Want it soupier? a: Extra cup of broth at the start. Same recipe otherwise. Thinner consistency. More like a soup than a stew."
