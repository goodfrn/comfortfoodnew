## What You Need for Sweet Potato Cookies

One cup of mashed sweet potato — cooked already, not raw. Use canned if fresh isn't on hand. Both work.

Brown sugar and white sugar, half and half basically. A cup of brown, half cup white. The brown keeps them soft.

Butter — three quarters cup, softened. Room temperature matters because cold butter doesn't mix right. Sits out 30 minutes if you're in a rush.

Two eggs. Just need them whole, beaten loosely. Nothing fancy.

Flour's two and a quarter cups. Sift it if you have a sifter. Whisk it if you don't. Lumps make the texture weird.

Baking soda. One teaspoon. That's the leavener.

Vanilla extract. A teaspoon. Pure, not imitation. Imitation tastes thin.

Walnuts — a cup, chopped fine. Toasted if you can grab them. Raw works, but toasted has more going on.

Turbinado sugar for the top, optional. The big crystals look pretty and add crunch.

## How to Make Sweet Potato Cookies

Heat the oven to 340°F first. Line a baking sheet — parchment or silicone. Skip this step and they stick. Not worth it.

Pour brown sugar, white sugar, flour, and baking soda into a big bowl. Whisk it together, breaking up any brown sugar clumps. Takes maybe two minutes. You'll feel when the lumps are gone.

Add the sweet potato, eggs, softened butter, vanilla. Stir until it comes together — everything visible gets incorporated. The dough looks wet but it holds its shape when you scoop it. That's right.

Use a tablespoon scoop. Drop each one onto the sheet with space between them. They spread a bit during bake, maybe half an inch wider. Don't crowd.

Sprinkle extra walnuts on top if you want texture. A pinch of turbinado sugar too. Makes it look intentional.

## Sweet Potato Cookies Bake and Cool

Slide the sheet in at 340°F. Set a timer for 13 minutes, but don't wander off.

Watch the edges. They'll turn golden and firm up while the center stays soft. That's the target — edges set, middle giving slightly. About 15-17 minutes usually, depends on your oven. Mine runs hot so I pull at 14.

Cool them on the sheet for two minutes. Then move to a wire rack. If you skip the rack and leave them on the hot sheet, they keep cooking underneath and get hard.

Don't stack them warm. Steam builds between them and softens everything. Wait until they're room temperature. Patience here actually changes the final texture.

## Sweet Potato Desserts Tips and Common Mistakes

The baking soda amounts to one teaspoon — that's your entire leavener. It's not much because sweet potato adds moisture already. Too much and they taste soapy.

Brown sugar matters more than you'd think. It keeps these chewy. White sugar alone makes them cakey. Don't skip the brown.

Storage is three days in an airtight container. After that they're still fine but the chewy part goes brittle. Nothing wrong with brittle, just different texture.

Actually, they freeze. A month or more. Thaw at room temperature and they're basically fresh again.

The sweet potato has to be mashed fully — no chunks. Chunks bake weird and uneven. Use canned if fresh is a whole thing.

Some people add chocolate chips. Never tried it that way. Seems like you're chasing a different cookie at that point.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Sweet Potato Cookies

**Can I use canned sweet potato instead of fresh?**
Yes. Drain it well if it's packed in liquid. Works the same as mashed fresh. Nobody will know the difference.

**How do I know when they're done baking?**
Edges firm up and turn golden. The center looks soft but not wet — like it would hold together if you picked it up. That's when you pull them. Takes 13-17 minutes at 340°F.

**Why do my cookies spread too much?**
Dough was probably warmer than it should be or the butter was too soft. Chill the scooped dough for 20 minutes before baking if that keeps happening.

**Can I make these sweet potato recipes without walnuts?**
Sure. Use the same amount of something else — pecans, chopped chocolate, nothing. They're still good. Walnuts just add a specific taste you might want.

**How long do these sweet potato baking recipes keep?**
Three days in a container at room temp. Longer in the fridge. A month frozen. After day three they're drier but still edible.

**What makes them chewy instead of cake-like?**
Brown sugar and the sweet potato itself keep moisture in. Also don't overbake them — pull when the edges set but the center still looks slightly underdone. Sounds wrong but it's how you get that texture.

**Can I make these into sweet potato pound cake or another dessert?**
Not really from this recipe — the ratio's built for cookies. But sweet potato desserts come in a hundred versions. This one's the cookie version.

**Do I need to sift the flour?**
Helps if you do. Breaks up lumps and makes the texture more uniform. Whisking works too if you don't have a sifter.
