instructions:
- "=== How to Boil Snow Crab Legs ==="
- "1. Big pot or Dutch oven? Depends on quantity. A decent Dutch oven fits about 0.45 kg at most; stock pot for whole batch. Avoid crowding – uneven cooking."
- "2. Fill pot half full with cold water. Add 1 1/4 tablespoons kosher salt. Briny but balanced, avoid fishy taste. Bring to a strong boil, hear the bubbling roar."
- "3. Drop crab legs carefully. Thawed? Simmer for roughly 3 1/2 minutes. Frozen? Closer to 5 1/2 minutes. Watch how shells brighten and steam billows, signals to check texture."
- "4. Lift out, place over paper towels on rimmed tray. Drain residual water stuck inside shells. Skip this, legs soggy, harder to crack cleanly. Let legs breathe before breaking them open."
- "=== How to Steam Snow Crab Legs ==="
- "5. Same big pot or Dutch oven trick. Don't crowd; if small pot, work in halves. Steamer basket mandatory. No it doesn’t work without."
- "6. Add cold water to just kiss the steamer basket’s rim. Salt down to 1-2 teaspoons kosher salt here. Less water means more steam pressure, keeps meat moist."
- "7. Place crab legs in steamer basket, cover tightly. Thawed? About 5 1/2 minutes steam time; frozen? Push to 7 1/2 minutes based on altitude & size. Smell steam, subtle ocean scent intensifies."
- "8. Remove with tongs to a platter. Residual heat cooks them slightly after removing. Avoid opening lid early – steam escape means dry crab meat."
- "=== How to Bake Snow Crab Legs ==="
- "9. Oven at 350°F. Toss crab legs in a deep baking dish or rimmed sheet—doesn’t need full single layer, pile is fine. Crusty edges will brown slightly as enclosed liquid steams."
- "10. Cover tightly with foil. Traps natural juices, steams crab legs gently. Bake thawed legs for 9-11 minutes. Watch foil’s slight ballooning; gas escapes pressure, a subtle timing hint."
- "11. Remove foil carefully—hot steam burst. Serve immediately. Watch legs plump and shells take on vivid orange-red tone."
- "=== How to Grill Snow Crab Legs ==="
- "12. Preheat grill medium heat. Spritz or brush grates with oil to avoid sticking. Crab shells can catch fire if too dry."
- "13. Lay legs right on grill grates. Lid closed to trap heat. Heat through 3-4 minutes. Listen for subtle crackling sounds, aromatic char smoke."
- "14. Flip once mid-way or rotate if flames flare. Char adds smoky note but don’t overcook—shells harden, meat dries quickly."
- "15. Remove and serve hot. Good on its own but a lemon garlic butter dip is killer."
- "Try each method, tweak salt and timing by crab size and freezer time. Crabby but rewarding."

ingredientsNote: "Coarse kosher salt is the standard, but sea salt works; fine salt dissolves too fast, can over-salt. Adjust salt depending on desired brininess and crab freshness. Water—not too much to cover legs entirely is better, especially for steaming to force even heat. Skip butter in cooking; add after, butter can choke steam. For foil baking, foil quality matters—thin foil tears and loses steam. Oil or coconut spray the grill well or risk sticking/cracking shells. Frozen crab legs best fully thawed overnight, but if time-restricted, slight extra cooking compensates. When in doubt, hold off cracking shells until just before eating, crab meat dries out fast once exposed."
instructionsNote: "Preheat necessary before adding crab legs—cold water boil takes longer, encourages overcooking trying to chase the boil. Use a rimmed tray with paper towels to catch drips, prevents slippery hands and soggy meat post-cook. Don’t overcrowd pots—crab legs need surface space for even heat. Adjust times slightly ±20 seconds based on size and freshness; no one lies exactly in cookbook times. Steam cooking demands tight lids to harness heat and moisture, open lid and meat turns leathery. For grill, medium heat avoids burnt shells or rubbery insides. Tent foil loosely once baked but allow steam release to prevent soggy texture. Always trust color and smell signals over a timer; orange-pink shells turning bright and steaming richly mean done. If unsure, better undercook briefly and reheat gently than risk tough, dry legs."

For steaming, keep water just below steamer basket rim—more pressure, better heat distribution."
