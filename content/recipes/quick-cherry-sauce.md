## Cherry Sauce Ingredients

Four cups of fresh cherries, pitted and halved. Canned works too. Doesn't taste as good, but it works.

Tupelo honey. Not regular honey. It dissolves smoother, thinner. Regular honey gets thick fast, traps air, turns grainy. Tried it. Tupelo stays glossy.

Two teaspoons of fresh lemon juice — acidulates the cherry, makes it pop. Bottled doesn't hold up. Fresh only.

Pinch of fine sea salt. Coarse salt doesn't dissolve right. Stays grainy. Fine sea salt melts in.

One tablespoon of cornstarch. Thickens without making it gloopy. Tapioca starch works too. Flour doesn't — turns cloudy.

Water or cherry brandy for thinning. If the sauce gets too thick while cooling — and it will — add a teaspoon of water and stir hard. Brandy if you want that underlying warmth. Skip it and it's still fine.

## How to Make Cherry Sauce

Heat everything together on medium. Cherries, honey, lemon, salt, cornstarch. Don't mix first. Just dump and stir once it starts warming. Honey melts into the cherry juice. Takes about five minutes. The mixture should bubble gently — not aggressively. Aggressive heat splits the sauce.

You're listening for that faint pop-pop sound. That's cherry skins releasing. You're watching the spoon. When liquid clings instead of sliding off the side, you're close.

## Getting the Right Thickness on Cherry Sauce

Simmer for 8 to 17 minutes depending on your cherries. Some cherry types release more liquid. Some are drier. You can't go by a timer here — you go by feel.

The sauce darkens. Bright red shifts to ruby. The surface stops rippling so fast. Smell gets deeper, less bright. That's your signal it's done thickening.

One time it got too thick. Like, totally seized. Added water a teaspoon at a time and stirred off the heat until it loosened. Never add liquid while it's still hot or it breaks and splits into grainy mess.

If you're using cherry brandy, fold it in after you kill the heat. Kirsch, Chambord, regular brandy — whatever. Adds that subtle warmth. Alcohol burns off, flavor stays.

## Cherry Sauce Tips and Common Mistakes

Don't stir constantly. Stir gently, persistently. Half-asleep kind of stir. Aggressive stirring breaks down the fruit too fast, turns it to paste.

Cherry type changes everything. Bing cherries are sweeter, need less time. Sour cherries need the full 17 minutes. Know what you're working with going in.

Cool it slightly before serving. It thickens more as it cools. If it looks too thin on the heat, wait. It won't be.

Storage: fridge for two weeks, maybe three. Freezer for months. Thaw overnight. Tastes the same.

Use it on pancakes, ice cream, roast duck, cheesecake, yogurt, oatmeal. Spread on a pastry with cream. Mix into sparkling water. Don't overthink it.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Cherry Sauce

**Can I use frozen cherries instead of fresh?**
Yeah. Thaw them first. The liquid they release is extra, so use less water when thinning. Texture's softer but it tastes fine.

**What if my sauce doesn't thicken?**
Cook it longer. Cornstarch needs heat to actually set. If it's been 17 minutes and still runny, the cherries had too much water. That happens. Just simmer another few minutes. Cornstarch does the work eventually.

**Can I skip the cornstarch?**
Not really. Without it you get cherry juice with fruit floating in it, not sauce. Tastes good but doesn't coat anything.

**How do I know when it's actually done?**
Spoon test. Dip a spoon in. Run your finger across the back. If your finger's path stays clear, it's thick enough. If it fills back in, keep going.

**Can I use honey from a bottle instead of tupelo?**
Clover honey works. Wildflower works. Tupelo just melts cleaner, stays glossy. If that's what you have, use it.

**Is this actually a pasta sauce or are the keywords lying?**
It's not pasta sauce. It's cherry sauce. Works on desserts, meat, breakfast. The keywords are doing a lot of heavy lifting.
