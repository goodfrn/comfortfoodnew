## What You Need for Creamy Chicken and Gnocchi Soup

Butter—three tablespoons, unsalted. The base of everything.
Yellow onion, medium. One. Diced. Not thin. Chunks that soften but still hold shape.
Two medium carrots and two celery stalks. Diced the same size as the onion. Matters for how they cook.
Four garlic cloves minced fine. You'll feel these if they're chunks.
All-purpose flour. Three tablespoons. This is your thickener, no cornstarch substitutes here.
Two cups of cooked chicken. Shredded. Grilled rotisserie chicken is fine—actually faster and better flavor.
Italian seasoning. One teaspoon. Mix it yourself if you have oregano, basil, and thyme. Bottled works too.
Ground nutmeg. Just 1/8 teaspoon. Sounds small. It is. But it changes everything—keeps the warmth subtle but noticeable.
Low sodium chicken broth. Five cups. Sodium control matters because you're adding Parmesan and salt later.
Olive oil. Two tablespoons. Separate from the butter. For the gnocchi pan.
One pound of potato gnocchi. Frozen or fresh. Store-bought. This recipe skips the homemade version.
Parmesan cheese. Half cup. Freshly grated, not the green container. Huge difference.
Kosher salt and white pepper. Adjust to taste—you'll know when it's enough.
Whole milk. One cup. Heavy cream, half a cup. Together they make it thick and rich without being heavy.
Fresh baby spinach. Three cups. Wilts in seconds.
Lemon zest. One teaspoon finely grated. From a fresh lemon, the peel only—no white pith.

## How to Make Creamy Chicken and Gnocchi Soup

Melt the butter in a heavy pot over medium heat. Once it's foamy, the onion, carrots, celery, and garlic go in at the same time. You'll hear a gentle sizzle. Let it sit undisturbed for maybe 30 seconds so the pan actually cooks them instead of just steaming. Then stir occasionally. The onion should turn translucent but not soft. The carrots need to soften but still have some bite left. This takes around 5 or 6 minutes. Don't rush it.

When the veggies look right, sprinkle the flour over everything evenly. Stir hard. It'll look like a rough wet paste for a moment—that's the roux forming and that's exactly what you want. Let it toast for just a minute until the raw flour smell goes away. You're not browning it. You're cooking off the raw taste. This step is what prevents the soup from turning grainy later, which honestly matters more than most people think about it.

Add the chicken chunks and Italian seasoning. Add that tiny pinch of nutmeg—it sounds small but it does something specific to the warmth of the soup. Pour the chicken broth in slowly while you stir. No rush. The roux will break apart as the liquid hits it and your stirring keeps lumps from forming. Keep stirring until it's all combined. Bring it to a low simmer—gentle bubbles breaking the surface, not a rolling boil—and let it sit for about 9 to 11 minutes. The flavors are melding. You can feel the soup getting better even though nothing visible changed much.

## How to Get This Soup Creamy and Rich

While the broth is simmering, heat the olive oil in a separate large skillet. Dump the gnocchi into the pan in a single layer. Let it sit undisturbed for 2 to 3 minutes. This is where the magic happens—the bottom gets a golden crust while the top stays soft. Flip each piece. Crisp the other side the same way. This adds actual texture contrast to a soup that would otherwise be entirely soft. Set the browned gnocchi aside on a plate. Don't overcrowd the pan while cooking them or they'll steam instead of sear and you'll lose that crust.

Once the broth has simmered and thickened slightly—it won't be thick yet, but you can feel it starting to hold together—stir in the Parmesan cheese, salt, and white pepper. The cheese melts into the broth and makes it sticky and creamy. Pour in the milk and heavy cream. Stir it in gentle. Return the soup to a faint simmer—and this is important—watch it carefully. Higher heat and dairy together means curdling. Lower the flame if it starts bubbling too hard. Just keep it barely moving.

Add the fresh spinach. It wilts fast. Watch for deep green but still with that pop of springy texture. Toss in the browned gnocchi last. This way the gnocchi stays crispy instead of getting soaked. Grate the lemon zest over the top right before serving. The tiny oils in the peel brighten the heavy cream base—makes it taste lighter even though it's not.

## Creamy Chicken Gnocchi Soup Tips and Common Mistakes

Serve it immediately. Soup thickens on standing because the gnocchi keeps releasing starch into the broth. If you need to reheat it the next day, do it gently. No microwave blasts. No high heat. Low flame and a splash of extra broth or milk as it warms up keeps the textures intact. If it's too thick, add milk gradually until it looks right again.

Fresh spinach is preferred but kale works if that's what you have. Add kale earlier and cook it longer to soften it down. Shredded rotisserie chicken slots in effortlessly and actually tastes better than plain cooked chicken. If you're using raw chicken, cook it in the broth first until it's opaque before moving forward. The cream of chicken gnocchi soup base stays the same.

Store-bought gnocchi is fine. Homemade is delicious but takes longer and this recipe is already pretty fast. Pan-searing the gnocchi—whether store-bought or homemade—is a huge upgrade over boiling it. Most people boil theirs. Don't. The difference is noticeable.

Watch the broth temperature carefully when you add the dairy. A spike in heat and you've got a grainy texture instead of smooth. Temperature tweaks everything here. Thick heavy cream is your safety valve if the milk alone looks weak. Add salt gradually and taste as you go instead of dumping it all in at the beginning. You'll end up with better control. Lemon zest is variable—freshen it if you want, but avoid the white pith or it gets bitter.

The recipe for creamy chicken gnocchi soup works with half and half instead of the milk and cream combination. Half and half gives you the same thickness with one ingredient instead of two. The flavor stays the same.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Chicken and Gnocchi Recipe

**Can I use frozen spinach instead of fresh?**
Yes. Squeeze out all the water first or it'll make the soup watery. Same amount works fine.

**How long does this keep?**
Three or four days in the fridge. The gnocchi gets softer each day but the soup stays good. Freezing breaks the texture of the gnocchi so don't bother.

**What if I don't have heavy cream?**
Half and half works. So does full whole milk if you use 2 cups of it instead of one milk plus half. Not as rich but still decent.

**Can I make this ahead and reheat it?**
Yeah. Make it, cool it, refrigerate it. Reheat gently on low heat with a splash of milk or broth stirred in. The gnocchi will absorb liquid overnight so it needs thinning.

**Does the lemon zest actually matter?**
It does. Sounds tiny but it's the only thing that cuts through the richness. If you don't have a lemon, skip it instead of subbing something else.

**Can I use fresh thyme instead of Italian seasoning?**
Use about a teaspoon of fresh thyme plus a pinch of dried oregano. Fresh thyme is stronger so less goes further. Bottled Italian seasoning is faster though.

**What's the difference between this and cream of chicken gnocchi soup from restaurants?**
Mostly salt and portion size. Restaurant versions are saltier. Yours is cleaner tasting. If you want it to taste like the olive garden creamy chicken gnocchi soup, add more salt and cream.
